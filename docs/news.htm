Culture and curriculum at a Tompkinsville school     

New facility caters to Islamic children, reflecting the Island's expanding diversity

 

By Marjorie Hack

Advance Staff Writer

31 October 1999

 

Like most first-grade boys on Staten Island, Muhammed Vardar and 
Arvi Duka are learning all about colors and counting and sounding out words.
They play with big trucks and learn to sing jolly songs that leave them 
giggling with pride and delight. But unlike their peers elsewhere, 
Muhammed, 7, of South Beach, and Arvi, 6, of Concord, are students 
at a brand new school in Tompkinsville that caters to Islamic families, 
and the boys are learning to do these things not only in English, but 
also in Arabic. The school is called the Islamic Elementary School and 
it is located in the Albanian Islamic Cultural Center in Tompkinsville. 
 
It is the first school of its kind on the Island in recent memory, 
although a Muslim-oriented after-school program operated out of PS 16,
 also in Tompkinsville, a few years ago. The bulk of the school's 
curriculum comes straight out of frameworks outlined by the New York State 
Education Department. In first grade for instance, students in the 
school will work at sorting and grouping objects, learn how to work
and play cooperatively and begin developing the concept of community, 
just like their peers in PS 29 in Castleton Corners or Our Lady Star of 
the Sea in Huguenot. But this school, which, like any other private school, 
is registered with the state and operated with private tuition money, 
provides another side to the school day of its 14 start-up students - a side 
that Principal Khurshid Khan hopes will keep them rooted in the Koran and the 
morals of Mohammed. Tuition is about $250 a month. "It's cultural training.
